# Domain 5: Cyber Incident Management and Resilience

Cyber resilience encompasses both planning and testing to maintain and recover
ongoing operations during and following a cyber incident. A financial
institution's ability to withstand and recover from a cyber incident is a
significant determinant of how a cyber event affects the institution and its
customers.

## Assessment Factor: Incident Resilience Planning and Strategy

### Planning

Incident response plans establish and communicate the capabilities to prepare
for, respond to, and recover from significant cyber events in a timely manner.
Effective planning includes the identification of events and the activities
required to respond. Planning also addresses how a financial institution manages
the response activities, including the roles, responsibilities, communication,
and reporting required during and after an event.

- Baseline: The institution has documented how it will react and respond to
  cyber incidents.
- Evolving: The remediation plan and process outlines the mitigating actions,
  resources, and time parameters.
- Intermediate: The incident response plan is designed to prioritize incidents,
  enabling a rapid response for significant cybersecurity incidents.
- Advanced: The incident response process includes detailed actions and
  rule-based triggers for automated response.
- Innovative: The institution is leading efforts to develop incident response
  exercises and scenarios that are shared across the sector.

### Testing

Testing the incident response plan is essential to understanding its
effectiveness. Testing identifies gaps, validates assumptions, and familiarizes
personnel with their responsibilities. Testing may take many forms, including
tabletop exercises, functional tests, and full-scale tests.

- Baseline: Scenarios are used to improve incident detection and response.
- Evolving: Recovery scenarios include plans to recover from data destruction
  and impacts to data integrity, data loss, and system and data availability.
- Intermediate: The institution corrects root causes for problems discovered
  during cybersecurity resilience testing.
- Advanced: Resilience testing is comprehensive and coordinated across all
  critical business functions.
- Innovative: The institution tests the ability to shift business processes or
  functions between different processing centers or technology systems for
  cyber incidents without interruption to business or loss of productivity.

## Assessment Factor: Detection, Response, and Mitigation

### Detection

Detection is the discovery of any event that has the potential to impact the
confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information or systems. Incident
detection focuses on discovering events through monitoring and reviewing
alerts, logs, and reports. Identifying whether an event is a true incident is
the next step in the detection process.

- Baseline: Alert parameters are set for detecting information security
  incidents that prompt mitigating actions.
- Evolving: Incidents are detected in real-time through automated processes
  that include instant alerts to appropriate personnel who can respond.
- Intermediate: Incidents are detected using sophisticated automated methods
  which result in rapid incident alerting, triage, and response.

### Response and Mitigation

Once a cyber incident is declared, response and mitigation procedures
determine the institution's actions. Response and mitigation activities seek
to limit impact to the institution and its customers. Activities include
communications, containment, eradication, and recovery efforts aligned with
the incident response plan.

- Baseline: Appropriate steps are taken to contain and control an incident to
  prevent further unauthorized access to or use of customer information.
- Evolving: Analysis of events is used to improve the institution's security
  measures and policies.
- Intermediate: Processes are in place to help contain incidents and restore
  operations with minimal service disruption.

## Assessment Factor: Escalation and Reporting

Escalation and reporting procedures ensure that key stakeholders — including
senior management, the board of directors, regulators, law enforcement, and
customers where appropriate — receive timely and accurate information about
cyber incidents and the institution's response.

- Baseline: A process exists to contact personnel who are responsible for
  analyzing and responding to an incident.
- Evolving: Employees that are essential to mitigating the risk (e.g., fraud,
  business continuity, corporate communications) know their role in incident
  escalation.
- Intermediate: A thorough and well-coordinated process is used to inform
  management, law enforcement, regulators, and other appropriate entities.
